Causal Discovery
on World Development
Indices

Does an increase in government spending cause a decrease in income inequality? Or is it the other way around? Does increasing health spending lead to children being more likely to stay in school? Does economic freedom lead to world happiness? What does it even mean for one indicator to "lead" to another?

Correlation

When we think of exploring index data, we often think of looking at correlations - does an increase in something lead to an increase or decrease in something else? Or is there no pattern in sight? 29 world development indices blow up into 406 pairwise comparisons - explore some of the correlations yourself by hovering over edges.

Noise

Trying to pick out one particular edge in a graph of all possible connections can feel as frustrating as trying to decide which questions to ask of your data from the space of all possible questions. Make things easier on yourself by filtering the absolute value of the correlation coefficients to get rid of the very weak correlations.

Causation

Everyone who works with data has heard the mantra: correlation does not equal causation. But - without proving anything per se - what if you could infer causation out of a network of correlations? Two correlations don't necessarily mean anything, but three correlations start to give you a basis for comparisons. For example, consider three variables: cigarettes per day, incidence of lung cancer, and some kind of addictive personality quotient. All three are positively correlated with each other, but you notice that controlling for cigarettes per day actually makes the association between lung cancer and having an addictive personality just about random. This is means having an addictive personality will only lead to lung cancer if it leads you to smoking a lot, and from that, you can now draw a causal edge from smoking more cigarettes a day to getting lung cancer. Running the world development indices through a causal discovery algorithm (IC) reveals a number of interesting, potentially causal, relationships. Caveat emptor, though, this doesn't prove anything, it's just a way for networks of conditional probabilities to point your experiments in the right direction.

Meaning

So what does it mean for an increase in school life expectancy to lead to an increase in the human development index or for there to be no significant causal connections between the percent of women MPs and the other indices? Honestly, it's hard to say. But if you're going to ask questions on a limited bandwidth, the black edges aren't a bad place to start.
