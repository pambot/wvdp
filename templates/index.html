Causal Discovery
on World Development
Indices

Does an increase in government spending cause a decrease in income inequality? Or is it the other way around? Does increasing health spending lead to children being more likely to stay in school? Does economic freedom lead to world happiness? What does it even mean for one indicator to "lead" to another?

Correlation

When we think of exploring index data, we often think of looking at correlations - does an increase in one indicator lead to an increase or decrease in another? Or is there no pattern in sight? 29 world development indices blow up into 406 pairwise comparisons - explore some of the correlations yourself by hovering over edges.

Noise

To get meaning from data, we need a way to filter it for what we want to see. Click on an indicator node to see only the connections associated with it. Most pairwise comparisons don't yield any significant patterns. Use the slider to filter out weak correlations. When you're done selecting a single node, click outside of the node to unselect it.

Causation

Correlation does not equal causation, but what if a network of correlations could highlight some potential causal relationships? Using the IC* algorithm, we can test if any two indicators are still dependent on each other even after factoring out all the surrounding indicators. When all the networks that fit the data can agree on a causal direction, we can draw an arrow, but if they cannot, we leave it as an undirected edge. Browse the categories of inferred causal edges using the selection boxes.

Meaning

How do we go from browsing data to finding meaning? We can use this spider's web of correlations as a launching point, but ultimately meaning will come from in-depth investigations that look at the people behind the numbers. If you're curious about inferring causal networks, there are great resources online.
